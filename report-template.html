Analyst Notes — Surprising Findings

IBM — The Biggest Surprise. IBM has been marketing itself as an AI company for over a decade, with Watson being one of the most advertised AI products in history. Yet their 10-K tells a completely different story — generic buzzwords, zero specific implementations, no metrics. This is perhaps the most striking example of the gap between marketing and reality in our dataset.

JPMorgan — Deliberately Vague? JPM scored 10/100, which initially seems shocking for a bank that's publicly committed $17B+ to technology annually. However, large financial institutions often deliberately keep AI strategy out of public filings for competitive intelligence reasons — they don't want competitors knowing exactly how they're using AI in trading, risk models, or fraud detection. Low filing score ≠ low actual AI adoption here.

Veeva — Classic AI Washing. Veeva is a $30B healthcare software company. They only mention AI in the context of regulatory risk (EU AI Act compliance) rather than as a business driver. Their exact words: "recently began incorporating AI capabilities into certain solutions" — with zero specifics. This is textbook AI washing from a company that should be AI-first by now.

Walmart — Interesting Outlier. WMT scored 24/100 which seems low, but for a traditional retailer this actually makes sense. Their AI use is operational (supply chain, inventory, personalization) rather than product-defining. The filing vagueness may reflect genuine early-stage adoption rather than washing.

// How This Works

01 — Data Source Pulls actual 10-K filings directly from SEC EDGAR. No summaries or secondary sources — raw legal documents.
02 — Text Extraction Strips 500k+ character HTML filings down to clean text, removing XBRL financial tags and boilerplate.
03 — AI Analysis Claude analyzes the most AI-relevant sections and scores on 5 dimensions with specific evidence.
04 — Scoring Specificity, Financial Impact, Integration Depth, Competitive Moat, Execution Evidence — each 0–10.
